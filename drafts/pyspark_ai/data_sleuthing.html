0. Executive Summary

In which we try to perform the sacred task of Exploratory Data Analysis using a combination of Spark and LLM’s through a new stack launched by DataBricks called PySpark. We will use this to try and visualize the top 50 longest train services in the world using PySpark, Google Search and GPT 4.

You will see us fail. As always, that’s a teachable moment.

1. Introduction 1

Let me first caveat this: I’m more of a planes person than a train person. Aviation analytics, particularly with open-source data is such a vast topic that I can spend days talking about it. I do just that, in fact, 9 AM to 6PM, five days a week, sometimes more; it’s my job after all. Which is why I’m not going to talk about planes here.

Instead, let’s talk about trains. Trains are all about slow travel, about watching the world go by idly through various landscapes amidst banter and making new friends. I lived next to trains, both in Hyderabad in my childhood, and later in Singapore in my adult life, at least until they stopped plying in 2011. Indeed, it’s been a regret of mine that I couldn’t get to travel on some routes I’ve always romanticised before they shut them down. All good reasons why it’s fun to day-dream which trains to take, and for how long. If we were to day-dream, why not consider the longest passenger train services in the world? Let’s use AI for this crucial task.

2. PySpark AI

2.1 Big Data Meets LLM’s

Databricks announced last month that they were launching a new SDK to “enrich” your Spark experience through English language queries. So instead of writing in SQL or PySpark, or indeed, getting GitHub Copilot to suggest queries in SQL or PySpark, you could now write directly in English. By connecting Google search API, Langchain and GPT 4 together, you could ask queries in English to get pie charts and such.

The stated premise is compelling. By typing just this2:

from pyspark_ai import SparkAI
import os
import openai

spark_ai = SparkAI()
spark_ai.activate()
# cars_df = spark_ai.create_df("2022 USA national auto sales by brand")
# cars_df.ai.plot()

You could get the Python kernel to traverse the web to retrieve the data, parse it, and plot it for you.
